# Visitor Journey Simulation — Edstellar Corporate Training Pricing Page
**Simulated as: Decision-maker arriving at this page for the first time**
**Date:** 2026-04-19

---

## How This Document Works

I am simulating 4 real buyer types — each with a different motivation, question set, and patience level. For each visitor I walk through the page section by section, mark what gets answered, what creates friction, and where they are most likely to leave. At the end is a consolidated gap analysis and ordering audit.

## Section-by-Section Journey

### S01 — Hero

**What it delivers:**
"Corporate Training Prices and Packages" headline. Three bullet-point promises: flexible packages, transparent pricing, custom quotes. Two CTAs: View Pricing / Talk to Expert.

| Visitor | Question at this point | Answered? | Risk |
|---------|----------------------|-----------|------|
| A | "Is this a pricing page or a brochure?" | ✅ Clear | Low |
| B | "Do they serve large enterprise?" | ⚠️ "60+ global enterprise clients" stat is below the fold | Medium |
| C | "Do they train healthcare companies?" | ❌ Nothing industry-specific in hero | **HIGH — may leave immediately** |
| D | "Do they have IT / cybersecurity training?" | ⚠️ Not explicit in hero, only implied by "2,000+ courses" | Medium |

**Gap:** The hero treats all visitors identically. A visitor from a specific industry (C) or domain (D) gets no immediate signal that Edstellar serves their niche. One sentence — "Trusted by IT, BFSI, Healthcare, Manufacturing and 20+ industries" — would reduce bounce for C and D.

---

### S02 — Client Logo Wall

**What it delivers:**
Visual trust — logos of clients who have used Edstellar.

| Visitor | Question at this point | Answered? | Risk |
|---------|----------------------|-----------|------|
| A | "Do companies like mine use them?" | ⚠️ Logos without visible company names reduce recognition | Medium |
| B | "Do they serve BFSI / enterprise?" | ❌ Cannot tell from anonymous logos | High |
| C | "Have healthcare companies used them?" | ❌ Same issue | High |
| D | "Do tech companies use them?" | ❌ Same issue | Medium |

**Gap:** Logos without names are nearly useless for trust-building in B2B. At minimum, showing names below logos — or replacing 2–3 logos with recognizable names in text — would increase credibility. A line like "Trusted by teams at Infosys, Tata, HDFC..." converts far better than anonymous rectangles.

---

### S03 — Industry Pricing Benchmark

**What it delivers:**
Market context — "$774 per learner globally," "60% cost savings with Edstellar," 2,000+ programs, 1,200+ trainers. Three content blocks on cost by company size, cost by delivery format, and how the Edstellar pricing model works.

| Visitor | Question at this point | Answered? | Risk |
|---------|----------------------|-----------|------|
| A | "What should I expect to pay for training?" | ✅ Well anchored | Low |
| B | "What's the cost-per-learner I can defend to my CFO?" | ✅ $774 benchmark is useful | Low |
| C | "Still haven't seen healthcare — is this for me?" | ❌ Generic content, no industry reference | **Medium — building impatience** |
| D | "What does IT training cost per session?" | ⚠️ Addressed generally, not IT-specifically | Low |

**Ordering observation:** This section correctly comes BEFORE the pricing table. Anchoring with market rates ($774/learner, $3,000–$7,000/session for ad-hoc ILT) before showing Edstellar's package model is strategically sound. It frames value before asking the visitor to evaluate packages.

**Gap:** Visitor C is now 3 sections in with zero healthcare signal. This is the last section before pricing — if C hasn't self-identified by now, they may not make it to S04.

---

### S04 — Pricing Tables ← CRITICAL SECTION

**What it delivers:**
3 tabs (All Included / IT & Technical / Non-Technical). 10-row comparison table: Starter / Growth / Enterprise / Custom with trainee licenses (120/320/800/Unlimited), hours, sessions, features, add-ons, footnotes.

**This is the section the entire page exists for. Every visitor arrives here with one question: "How much does it cost and what do I get?"**

| Visitor | Question at this point | Answered? | Risk |
|---------|----------------------|-----------|------|
| A | "Which plan covers 200 employees?" | ❌ **Critical gap** — 200 employees ≠ trainee slots. The model is licenses, not headcount. A visitor with 200 employees cannot map their size to Starter (120 slots) or Growth (320 slots) without understanding that 1 employee can use multiple licenses | **HIGH bounce** |
| B | "What does this cost in dollars?" | ❌ **No price is shown anywhere.** Every plan says "Enquire Now." A procurement manager comparing 3 vendors on a tight timeline will leave immediately for a vendor who shows pricing | **IMMEDIATE bounce** |
| C | "Do they have healthcare training programs?" | ✅ Partially — Non-Technical tab includes "Compliance" which covers healthcare compliance, but it's not explicit | Medium |
| D | "Does IT & Technical tab have cybersecurity?" | ⚠️ Tab says "1,000+ IT & Technical Skills" but doesn't list any domains | Medium |

**The biggest single problem on the page:**

> No price is shown. This is a "pricing page." Visitors come here to find a price. When every CTA says "Enquire Now" with no number next to it, two things happen: (1) self-service buyers bounce immediately to a competitor who shows pricing, (2) procurement managers cannot create a shortlist without a number.

**This does NOT mean you must show prices.** But you must fill the gap with something: a "starting from" indicator, a price range, a "price-on-request with 24-hour response" promise, or at minimum a clear statement of *why* prices aren't shown (because it's customized per industry/location). Right now there is silence — and silence reads as evasion.

**Second gap — the license model is non-intuitive:**
The footnotes explain that 1 license = 1 person × 1 day. But a visitor thinking in headcount ("I have 200 employees") cannot quickly calculate what they need. They need a bridge: "A team of 50 people trained twice a year on full-day courses = 100 licenses. That fits the Starter plan."

**Third gap — no mid-table social proof:**
After digesting the table, the visitor has no reassurance that other companies found this pricing reasonable. A single 1-line testimonial quote pulled into the pricing section ("We went from ad-hoc spending to a Growth plan — saved 40% in year one — Infosys L&D team") would dramatically reduce drop-off here.

### S05 — Package Comparison Guide (How to Choose)

**What it delivers:**
3-step guide: estimate team size → calculate training volume → match to package. Decision table matching company profile to plan.

| Visitor | Question at this point | Answered? | Risk |
|---------|----------------------|-----------|------|
| A | "So… which plan is right for 200 employees?" | ✅ This answers it — but only if A reached S05 | **A may have already bounced at S04** |
| B | "I already know my volume, I just need a price." | ❌ Still no price | Bounce risk remains |
| C | Scrolling past, still no healthcare context | ❌ | Impatience building |
| D | "Do I need Starter or Growth for 30 people?" | ✅ Step 1 addresses this | Low |

**Ordering problem:**
S05 should logically come BEFORE or be embedded INSIDE S04. A visitor confused by the table in S04 needs the "How to Choose" guide immediately — not one section later. By the time they scroll to S05, many have already made up their mind (or left).

**Recommendation:** Embed a collapsed "Help me choose" accordion or a simple 2-question selector (How many employees? How many sessions per year?) directly inside S04, above the table. S05 can remain as a detailed guide for those who want depth.

---

### S06 — ROI & Value Framing

**What it delivers:**
Business case for training investment — turnover cost reduction, productivity stats, research citations.

| Visitor | Question at this point | Answered? | Risk |
|---------|----------------------|-----------|------|
| A | "How do I justify this to my MD?" | ✅ Strong content | Low |
| B | "CFO wants ROI numbers — do you have them?" | ✅ Well served | Low |
| C, D | Still searching for industry/domain relevance | ❌ Generic framing | Low — most visitors accept generic ROI |

**Ordering observation:** Good position. After seeing the table and not getting a price, visitors who are still curious need a reason to stay. ROI framing provides that reason. However, placing it AFTER the table means the visitor has already decided whether to stay or leave — this section is too late to prevent bounce, it can only retain the already-committed.

---

### S07 — Delivery Modes & Pricing

**What it delivers:**
VILT vs onsite vs blended — pricing implications, what's included per mode.

**Observation:** This answers a question that most visitors haven't consciously asked yet — they're still processing whether the packages fit them. The delivery mode question typically arises at negotiation stage, not evaluation stage. Its position at S07 is reasonable.

**Gap:** The onsite pricing penalty (30% extra) is important. But it's buried in a mid-page section and repeated in footnote 4. Visitors who plan to do onsite training need to see this earlier — ideally in the pricing table itself, as a visible "note" row.

---

### S08 — What's Included vs Alternatives

**What it delivers:**
Comparison table: Edstellar vs In-House Team vs Ad-Hoc Vendors vs eLearning Platforms.

**Observation:** Excellent content, wrong position. Visitor B (procurement manager comparing vendors) needed this at S03-S04, not S08. By S08, a procurement manager who was going to stay is still here — but one who bounced earlier never saw this highly persuasive content.

**Ordering gap:** This section would be more effective immediately after the pricing table (S04), when the visitor is actively comparing options and asking "why not just hire a training manager?" Moving it to S05 position (and pushing the current S05 guide to inline within S04) would intercept the "why Edstellar over alternatives?" objection at the right moment.

---

### S09 — Trainer Quality Assurance

**What it delivers:**
1,200+ verified trainers, 4-step vetting process, domain depth.

**Observation:** Most B2B buyers have a latent fear: "What if I pay for a package and the trainer is bad?" This section addresses that fear well. Position at S09 is acceptable but slightly late — visitors who doubted quality may have left by now. A brief trainer credibility signal (not a full section) should appear near S04.

---

### S10 — Dedicated Learning Services Manager

**What it delivers:**
The LSM concept — 5 phases of support from onboarding to renewal, single point of contact.

**Observation:** This is actually a strong differentiator that most competitors don't offer. Yet it's at S10, where visitor attention is significantly reduced. Visitor A (L&D Manager) would find this enormously reassuring — but only if they're still reading. The LSM benefit is currently listed as row 6 in the pricing table (✓ checkmark only). It deserves a callout much earlier — a single sentence in the hero or immediately below the table.

---

### S11 — Client Outcomes / Case Studies

**What it delivers:**
Carousel of real training deployments with results — company type, training domain, outcome metrics.

**Ordering problem:**
Social proof at S11 is functionally invisible to most visitors. Research on B2B landing pages consistently shows that case studies placed BEFORE pricing increase conversion — they answer "has this worked for companies like mine?" before the visitor must evaluate price. Here they appear AFTER pricing, which means only already-convinced visitors see them.

**Recommendation:** Pull one case study (just the headline + outcome stat, not the full card) into S04 or immediately before S04. The full carousel can remain at S11 for visitors who want depth.

---

### S12 — Testimonials

**Same issue as S11.** Testimonials at S12 help SEO and retention of highly engaged visitors. They do not prevent bounce. At least one testimonial quote should appear near the pricing table or in the hero.

---

### S13 — Industry Coverage

**Critical ordering problem.**

Visitor C (healthcare HR Director) has been on this page for 10 sections with zero confirmation that Edstellar serves healthcare. By S13 she has either bounced or is skimming.

Industry coverage is a PRE-QUALIFICATION signal, not a post-conversion reassurance. It should appear at S02 or S03 — immediately after the hero — so visitors from specific sectors self-identify and commit to reading the pricing.

**What to move:** The industry grid (or a condensed 2-line version: "Deployed across IT, BFSI, Healthcare, Manufacturing, Retail, Energy, Telecom, Pharma, Government") belongs in the hero subtext or immediately after the logo wall.

---

### S14 — Platform Capabilities

**What it delivers:**
12-feature platform grid — scheduling, HRMS integration, analytics, certificate management, etc.

**Observation:** Platform features are a secondary decision factor for most buyers — but for IT heads and HR Directors at mid-to-large companies, HRMS integration and analytics may be dealbreakers. Placing this last means these buyers either scroll far or never see it.

**Gap:** The platform inclusion ("at no additional cost with every package") is itself a price-related benefit. It should be referenced — even as a one-liner — near the pricing table: "All packages include full access to the Edstellar training platform — scheduling, analytics, and HRMS integration — at no extra cost."
